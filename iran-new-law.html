Iran Intensifies Dress Code Penalties for Women

On Wednesday, Iran's legislature passed a bill that could see women who violate the nation's strict Islamic dress code face up to a decade in prison. This move comes amid a backdrop of increasing public defiance against the dress code, particularly following the tragic death of 22-year-old Mahsa Amini last year, who was arrested for allegedly breaching these regulations.

  • The bill, titled "Support for the Culture of Hijab and Chastity," is set for a trial run of three years. However, it still awaits the nod from the Guardian Council, a conservative assembly of clerics and jurists, to be enacted into law.
  • The proposed legislation aims to "protect society" and "strengthen family life," as stated by Iran's executive and judiciary in May. The country's ruling conservatives believe that any relaxation in the dress code would disrupt "social norms."
  • The bill stipulates that dress code violations linked to entities Iran perceives as antagonistic foreign forces would attract the harshest penalties. Women found collaborating with "foreign or hostile governments, media, groups, or organizations" in violating the dress code could be imprisoned for five to ten years. Similarly, those seen "half-naked in public spaces" could also face extended jail terms.
  • Presently, non-compliance with the dress code can result in a prison sentence ranging from 10 days to two months. The new bill also proposes a fine of five million rials (approximately $182) for women caught driving without a hijab or donning "inappropriate clothing." Additionally, media entities "promoting nudity" or "mocking the hijab" would be penalized, and business owners could face travel bans if their female employees breach the dress code.
  • Iran's ultraconservative President, Ebrahim Raisi, has been vocal about his intent to end the practice of women not wearing the hijab. This sentiment is echoed by the recent actions of authorities and police, who have amplified measures against those not adhering to the dress code, including shutting down non-compliant businesses and installing surveillance cameras in public areas to monitor violations.
  • The United Nations has expressed concerns over the bill, cautioning that its implementation could heighten the risks of violence, harassment, and arbitrary detention for women and girls. It's worth noting that since the 1979 Islamic revolution, women in Iran have been mandated to cover their head and neck.
  • To enforce this controversial ban, Education Minister Gabriel Attal announced a large-scale training program. By the end of 2023, 14,000 educational personnel in leadership positions will be trained, and this number will escalate to 300,000 by 2025. The scale and speed of this initiative have raised eyebrows, leading many to question the allocation of resources and the broader implications for educational institutions.
  • This development underscores the ongoing tension between traditionalist forces in Iran and a populace that is increasingly pushing back against restrictive norms.
