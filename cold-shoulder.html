Cutting Corners: The Cold Shoulder Conspiracy

Browsing the internet is a bit like navigating a bustling digital bazaar. Between pop-up ads showcasing ultra-thin models and clickbait headlines, I managed to stumble upon a curious thread—a fashion conspiracy that's been hiding, ironically, in plain sight.

No, it’s not pockets. It’s…the cold shoulder.

Meagen Kerr

It's everywhere, from high-end boutiques in Sydney to bustling street markets in Bali - at least in their plus size section, that is if they have one.

At first glance, it's just another trend. But as any fashionista knows, its never just a trend.

Historically, fashion has catered to the select few, leaving many on the outside looking in. This exclusivity isn't just about price tags or luxury brands; it's about representation, about who gets to see themselves reflected in the world of haute couture and who doesn't.

But here's where things get interesting:

A conspiracy theory suggests that instead of embracing the challenge of designing for diverse body types, some designers might have taken a shortcut. Why grapple with the intricacies of designing a pattern for arm when you can simply... cut out some fabric and call it a day?

Huh...the cold shoulder seems less like a style statement and more like a convenient detour.

Even though there is no concrete evidence that this actually happened the sheer popularlity of theory suggest that well, most people still feel left out of fashion industry.

Fashion should be a celebration of all bodies and styles. But more often than not, it feels like an exclusive club with a strict guest list, catering to a select few while leaving many others waiting at the door. It’s all very old-fashioned.

The cold shoulder conspiracy, whether true or not, serves as a poignant reminder of this exclusivity. It underscores the need for a more inclusive, empathetic, and genuinely creative fashion world—one where designs aren't just for the chosen ones but resonate with everyone.
